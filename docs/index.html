I.
A representation problem

One-hot says everything is unrelated.

A one-hot vector is all zeros except a single 1. The cosine between any two distinct one-hot vectors is zero — frock and dress are as orthogonal as frock and spaceship. Embeddings learn the geometry from text.

✕ One-hot

Identity, not meaning.

Every distinct pair gets cosine 0. The representation has no notion of semantic similarity — bag-of-words inherits the same blindness. Stemming and stop-words barely help.

✓ Sentence-transformer

Geometry encodes meaning.

Read these as a ranking, not a percentage. Sentence-transformer cosines mostly live in [0.2, 0.7]; 0.42 is a meaningful synonym signal, not "42% similar". Trained on >1B sentence pairs.

II.
A bake-off

Two queries. Two different winners.

TF-IDF beats embeddings on exact-token queries (product codes, brand names). Embeddings beat TF-IDF on paraphrase. Production search is a hybrid — union of candidates, then re-rank.

III.
A checklist

Three checks before you replace keyword search.

Skip these and you ship a system that feels smarter but quietly regresses on the queries that pay the bills.

1

Ground-truth queries?

A handful of (query, expected-product) pairs is the minimum. Without it you cannot measure top-1 or top-K accuracy; "it feels better" isn't an answer Marcus accepts.

✕ "Demo on my favourite query."✓ "8 hand-curated queries with expected products."
2

Precision@k and failures?

Headline accuracy hides failure modes. The query that returned a linen shirt at rank 1 instead of a sundress — that is the bug top-1 alone wouldn't surface.

✕ "Top-1 = 6/8 — ship it."✓ "6/8 top-1; inspected the two misses."
3

Exact-match & stale?

Product codes and brand names need TF-IDF, not embeddings. When a merchandiser rewrites a description, the cached embedding goes stale — re-encode nightly.

✕ "Embeddings everywhere, no fallback."✓ "TF-IDF + semantic union; nightly re-embed."

IV.
Check yourself

Seven questions from the field notebook.

Click to flip. Numbering matches lesson.md.

▸ Click to revealWhy can't bag-of-words match synonyms?
Each document is a vector of word counts indexed by a fixed vocabulary. frock and dress occupy different indices, so the query and the product vector share only the summer dimension — cosine is dominated by that single overlap. No notion that frock and dress refer to the same thing.
▸ Click to revealOne-hot(frock, dress) = 0 = one-hot(frock, spaceship). Why?
A one-hot vector is all zeros except a 1 at the word's index. Dot-product of two one-hots for distinct words is zero — no overlapping non-zero positions — so cosine is zero regardless of semantics. Identity, not meaning.
▸ Click to revealcosine(frock, dress) = 0.249. "Only 25%." Right?
No. Sentence-transformer cosines for this model mostly live in [0.2, 0.7]. 0.25 is not "25% similar" — it is a ranking signal well above the ~0.0 baseline for unrelated words. Read cosines as ranking, not percentage.
▸ Click to revealWhy a sentence-transformer rather than averaging word vectors?
Averaging throws away word order and weights every token equally; "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" get identical embeddings and high-information words are diluted by stop-words. A sentence-transformer is trained end-to-end to produce one vector capturing the whole sentence.
▸ Click to revealTop-1 = 6/8, top-5 = 7/8. Which to report?
Both. Top-1 is what customers experience when only looking at the top result. Top-5 reflects realistic browsing. The gap is headroom that re-ranking with popularity or filters could recover.
▸ Click to revealWhen does TF-IDF beat embeddings?
Wins: exact-token queries — product codes, SKUs, brand names. Loses: synonym-heavy or paraphrased queries. Production search uses both and merges candidate lists.
▸ Click to revealMerchandiser rewrites 3 descriptions. What breaks if you do nothing?
Cached embeddings reflect the old text. Customers can search using new-description language and never find those products. Fix: nightly re-embedding of any product whose updated_at changed in 24h, plus a small near-real-time job for new products.
